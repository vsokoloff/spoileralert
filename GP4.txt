Prototype Description
Prototype Overview and Details
Our low-fidelity prototype is a simulation of the mobile app component from our design proposal, which includes receipt scanning and recipe recommendations. We received feedback from the design proposals that the ability to scan a grocery receipt and populate the expiration date information automatically is one of the most important features to include, so we prioritized that functionality, along with recommending recipes and a general expiration overview of the current items the user has in their fridge.
The homepage (Figure 1) includes color-coded food cards that list out the different products a user has in their fridge, how many days they have left until it expires, and a label indicating the type of product it is. On this page, the user can easily check the status of their fridge and the items inside, giving them a visual representation of which items need to be used the quickest (red being the most urgent categorization, which only indicates a few days left or already expired). The user can also edit the item (Figure 2) if they want to change any information about the item. For example, if the user finds a different expiration date for the feta, they can click the pencil icon and change the date. If the user throws away the spinach due to it being expired, however, they can click the trash icon to delete it.

The add page (Figure 3) features two modes of data entry, including the receipt scanning feature that was highly recommended and desired amongst users (both from our initial study and from the poster feedback rounds). If a user comes back from the grocery store with a long list of items on their receipt, they can click the “Scan Receipt” button, which takes them to the scanning page (Figure 4). This will then load in the items from the user’s receipt and populate them with generic or brand-specific expiration dates. The user will also have the option to edit any part of the data extracted from the receipt after the scanning process. If the user chooses individual manual entry instead, they will be taken to the same page as the “Edit Item”, which includes all of the same fields (only blank).

The final feature in this first version of our prototype is SPOY (pictured below), which is an AI assistant designed to give recipe recommendations based on the foods in the user’s fridge. SPOY takes into consideration the most urgent items and curates different recipe ideas for the user. For example, the current demo does not have salmon in the fridge, so SPOY only responds to. If a user in our user group (college students) is running low on time and needs a quick meal using only the items they currently have (especially the ones that are expiring soon), they can converse with SPOY briefly to find a suitable recipe. They can prompt the AI model with as little or as much information as they want (in this case, just the word “Feta”), and receive a suggested recipe that uses feta and other ingredients currently in the user’s fridge. This urges the user to use the different ingredients in their fridge, especially the ones that are set to expire the quickest. With an easy, seamless way to get personalized recommendations for recipes, the user can feel more empowered to use their expiring ingredients and limit food waste.

Scenario of Use
Jordan is a 20-year-old third-year at the college living in an off-campus apartment with two roommates. Between his classes, part-time job, and study sessions, Jordan goes grocery shopping once a week at best, and cooking feels like a significant burden when it’s already 8 PM. Jordan throws out forgotten produce and half-used dairy items frequently, and spends his money on food that never gets eaten.
User Interaction with Spoiler Alert:
Post-Grocery Shopping Scan: Jordan returns from the grocery store with a receipt. He opens Spoiler Alert, taps “Add items” -> “Scan Receipt” and scans the receipt. Spoiler Alert parses the grocery items and automatically populates expiration dates. Jordan notices the app added a generic expiration date to the feta cheese and manually corrects it using the edit functionality, then saves.
Daily Fridge Check: Two days later, Jordan opens Spoiler Alert before class. The homepage shows spinach color-coded in red (“Expiring in 3 days”) and eggs labelled “Expiring today.” Without opening the fridge he knows what needs to be used immediately.
Recipe Suggestions: That same evening, Jordan only has 30 minutes before his scheduled study session. Rather than ordering takeout, Jordan asks SPOY to suggest a recipe using eggs and feta. SPOY returns a recipe for a baked feta and egg skillet using the ingredients already flagged as expiring. He cooks the meal, using both items, and avoids having to throw them away.
Category Modification: Jordan moves the leftovers from dinner to a storage container and changes the category from “Eggs & Dairy” to “Leftovers”, and updates the expiration date to be 3 days from that date.
Expiration Notifications: Two days later, Jordan receives a push notification that says: “Your leftover feta and egg skillet expires tomorrow.” He eats the remainder for lunch the day after. He wasted no food that week.
Design Rationale
We chose to create this prototype primarily because of the feedback that we received from both the user survey and the poster feedback rounds, in which the majority of users indicated a preference for receipt scanning and recipe recommendations. Since many reviewers in the feedback round suggested that the touchscreen fridge magnet would be costly and hard to implement, we chose to focus on the mobile app to minimize complications and focus on the most important features of Spoiler Alert. One feature that we chose to implement is receipt scanning, which was chosen as a result of multiple pieces of input (survey results, poster session feedback) suggesting that this feature was important to users. Our study suggested that several users wanted a way to scan in items that wasn’t too time-consuming while maintaining optional manual entry for correction. We then chose to include both options under the add page so that users who wanted a more efficient mode of data entry could choose that, while giving full control through the manual entry and edit options. Similarly, we received a lot of positive feedback about the recipe suggestion feature, and many college students remarked that they would be more inclined to cook and use up their ingredients if they had personalized recipe recommendations within the app. This then motivated SPOY, our chatbot segment of the app, which gives personalized meal suggestions. 
We determined three primary criteria that could be considered essential to the success of our prototype based on users’ cited needs and personal preferences of our team. The first criterion was that of functional clarity. We want users to be able to pick up the app and understand it with little to no intervention beyond the knowledge that it tracks what’s in your fridge. This pairs with the decrease in mental load necessary to use it; we don’t want to force users to remember countless specific functions. Secondly, we want the user to feel informed. Just as inundating the user with information is burdensome, we also want to avoid having too little information. If the user can gather what they need from quickly glancing at the home screen, they’ll feel that it’s more useful long-term. Third, it must be convenient. If the data entry of our app feels like it takes too much time or effort on the part of the user, they will not continue to use it. We need to ensure that it seamlessly transitions from entry, to edit, to home, with minimal input from the user unless they specifically want to include something. In our testing process, we will be seeking to know what information is superfluous, what other information users may want, and what feels slow or inconvenient about the entry process.
Results of In-Class Evaluation
What Worked
The core features of the prototype worked well for our peers, particularly the automation and AI components that enhanced the convenience of the data entry and recipe creation processes. Users generally "liked the bot" (quote written from our feedback note taker) and found the recipe recommendation concept engaging. It was noted that even with minimal instruction, participants could navigate the tabs and find the features they were looking for. Navigating to the “SPOY” page was a pleasant surprise for most users, and they noted that the chatbot made them more likely to use the app. Furthermore, receipt scanning was validated as "VITAL" and "most excellent" as a feature of Spoiler Alert. Participants confirmed it would be a preferred method for data entry to save time. Some said they may not be inclined to use the app without this feature. In terms of UI and visual experience, the use of tags and labels was viewed as a strong point for organization, and peers were pleasantly surprised that you could click on labels to see all items of that category. Finally, our peers were able to successfully find functionalities when they went looking for them, suggesting the basic functionalities are easily findable and intuitively placed. Still, some features were not as clearly labeled. We’ll discuss this in the next section. 
What Did Not Work
There was confusion between "location" labels (e.g., freezer) and "food type" labels (e.g., produce). This likely stems from the fact that both label types appear visually identical, giving users no immediate signal that they serve different organizational purposes. Users also expressed a desire to select more than one label at a time and for the option to potentially have an "other" label that was customizable, though one participant noted that this option might be more confusing to implement.
Furthermore, while the color-coding was liked, participants noted the need for a legend or key to explain exactly what the colors signified regarding expiration days. Without explicit guidance, users had to deduce meaning on their own, which adds cognitive load that contradicts our goal of functional clarity. Several testers also did not realize they could click on labels, likely because the labels lacked any visual affordance (maybe an underline or highlight) that would suggest interactivity. The lack of an "undo" button or confirmation step before deleting items was noted as a potential pain point, which is particularly risky given that accidental deletion would require the user to re-enter data manually. The users were very intrigued by SPOY, but in particular one tester questioned how the chatbot would handle ingredients not currently in the system, suggesting that the boundaries of SPOY's knowledge were not communicated clearly enough upfront. Finally, users pointed out that moving an item from the pantry to the freezer should automatically extend its expiration date— an expectation rooted in real-world food storage knowledge that our prototype had not yet accounted for.

Pros and Cons
Using Figma allowed us to create a relatively high-fidelity "medium" prototype that simulated the app experience effectively. It was particularly useful for identifying which specific features—like the receipt scanner—required more iteration before final implementation. As for cons, we think the prototype felt "isolated" from the real-world context of use. Participants were hesitant to try the receipt scanner, given that they didn’t have a receipt. While we had dummy data for this feature, users had to be prompted to try it out. Figma also made it difficult to simulate "failure cases" for the receipt scanner, such as how the app would handle blurry images or unrecognized brand names. We believe this will be the hardest part of our final prototype to implement. 
Future Changes
We will design a "first-time tutorial" or onboarding phase to ensure users know features like label-clicking and manual entry are available. We will also implement filtering for the main inventory page with "Urgency/Soonest to Expire” as the default setting. Additionally we’ll look into adding a search option and the ability to filter by specific categories, like produce. Recognizing our target demographic of college students, we will more seriously entertain adding the ability to tag roommates or share lists. Still, we only received these wants after prompting the users to express whether or not there were any additional features they might like. Given the interest in SPOY, we want to attempt to fine-tune the chatbot to automatically include items that are expiring soon in its suggestions and add the ability to save recommended recipes and chat history. Finally, given that our prototype was hosted on Figma instead of on a phone, we couldn’t test out a major part of our design - notifications. We will add notifications for expiring foods and recipe recommendations. Finally, we want to add an "undo" or "recently deleted" folder to prevent users from accidentally losing data. For evaluation, in retrospect, bringing physical props (actual food items or receipts) to better simulate the environment in which the app would be used would have been beneficial for our participants. We think we could have done a better job at simulating the environment in which our prototype would be used, especially considering that during our poster session, the hardware design stressed how much environment and location were important for effective use of notifications and reminders.
